Shifts and pay — retail

Built around how this shop actually runs.

A rostering and pay app for a family-run perfume business with two shopfronts. Every part of it exists because something was going wrong without it. Nothing here is a feature for the sake of a feature list.

The shop

Two shopfronts, a family, and a handful of staff.

A luxury perfume retailer with a store in Wollongong and a pop-up in Shellharbour. The people working the floor are family and hired staff, and they move between the two. Nobody sits at a desk. Nobody has a work email. Everything happens on a phone, usually mid-shift.

The scheduling tools you can buy assume an office, a rota that never changes, and one flat hourly rate. None of that was true here. What follows is what was actually breaking — and what was built for each of them.

Getting people paid

Payday used to start with a calculator.

What was happening

A Sunday is worth more than a Tuesday morning, and an evening is worth more than an afternoon. Every week, someone sat down and worked out by hand what each person was owed — hour by hour, at a different rate for each stretch.

What happens now

You enter the shift. The app prices it. It knows a Monday 3–9 crosses into evening rates at five, and splits it there. Saturdays, Sundays and public holidays price themselves.

What was happening

Public holidays are easy to forget — right up until payday, when someone realises the whole day should have been paid at a different rate.

What happens now

Add the holiday once, whenever you think of it. Every shift on that day is repriced automatically, and it's marked on the roster so the team sees it too.

What was happening

Give someone a raise, and last month's pay quietly changed with it. Numbers that had already been paid out stopped matching the numbers on the screen.

What happens now

A new rate starts on a date you choose. Shifts worked before that date keep the rate they were worked at. What you paid in June still reads as what you paid in June.

Who's on

The roster stopped living in a group chat.

What was happening

“Am I on Saturday?” was a text message. The roster existed in a group chat and in people's heads, and the two didn't always agree.

What happens now

Open the app: today, the week ahead, either shop. Who's on now, who's in later, who's already finished. Staff see the whole roster — they need to know who they're working with. They only ever see their own pay.

What was happening

It's easy to put someone on at both shops at once. You usually find out on the day, when one of the two doesn't get covered.

What happens now

A shift that clashes with one they already have won't save. The app tells you when and where the overlap is, before it becomes Saturday's problem.

What was happening

Most weeks look like last week. Rebuilding the same roster from scratch every time is busywork nobody has time for.

What happens now

Set a shift to repeat weekly and it fills the weeks ahead. Any week that would double-book someone is skipped, and it tells you which ones.

The team

Shaped around a family business, not an office.

What was happening

“Stocktake Sunday, both shops close at four” gets posted to the group chat once, and is buried under other messages by dinner.

What happens now

A notice sits at the top of the roster until each person dismisses it themselves. Instructions meant for one shift — open with Amira, bring the spare keys — ride on that shift, where they can't get lost.

What was happening

In a family business, the same name comes up more than once. Choosing who to roster meant guessing between three people called Tareq.

What happens now

Anyone can be hidden from the scheduling list without touching their shifts or their pay, and names can be set to whatever people actually call each other.

What was happening

Nobody has a company email address. Staff live on their phones, and a tool that opens with “create an account” doesn't get used.

What happens now

Send a link over WhatsApp. They pick their own username and password, and add it to their home screen — after that it opens like any other app on the phone.

What was happening

People come and go. When someone leaves, their access needs to go with them — but the record of what they worked, and what they were paid, can't.

What happens now

Switch them off and they're locked out the next time they open it. Their shifts and pay history stay exactly where they were, for the books.

In short

A tool that fits the shop, instead of a shop that fits the tool.

Roster does two things — who's working, and what that costs — and it does them the way this business actually works, down to the five o'clock boundary and the three people with the same first name.

It belongs to the business. No subscription, no charge per person, and nothing about the shop or its staff sitting on someone else's platform. The next stage takes the same tool further across the floor: stock, and orders.
